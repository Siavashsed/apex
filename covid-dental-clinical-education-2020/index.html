How COVID-19 Forced Dental Schools to Rebuild Clinical Education in 2020

Dr. Tofigh Sedighi on how the 2020 pandemic reshaped dental clinical education worldwide - and why some changes should stay permanent.

By March 2020, dental schools across the world had shut their clinic doors almost overnight, and nobody had a clear plan. I remember standing in my own practice in Mahabad on a Tuesday morning in late March, looking at a full appointment book and an empty waiting room, thinking about the third-year students I'd spoken with at a conference in Tehran just weeks earlier - students who were weeks away from completing clinical requirements they'd been building toward for years. The question everyone was asking wasn't theoretical: how do you teach clinical dentistry when you can't touch a patient?

The answer that emerged in 2020 wasn't clean or elegant. It was improvised, sometimes brilliant, occasionally absurd, and it forced dental education institutions to make decisions in weeks that would normally take years of committee debate. What happened to clinical dentistry education that year deserves an honest account - not a celebration, not a postmortem, but a clear-eyed look at what the field learned under pressure.

The Scale of Disruption Was Unlike Anything in Modern Dental Education

When the World Health Organization declared COVID-19 a pandemic on March 11, 2020, dental schools were already in crisis mode. Aerosol-generating procedures - which describes most of what dentists do - placed dental clinics among the highest-risk settings for transmission. The American Dental Association issued guidance suspending elective procedures, and institutions from London to Lahore followed. ADA guidance from March 2020 explicitly recommended that dentists postpone all non-emergency care, a directive that cascaded directly into clinical training programs.

The numbers were stark. A survey published in the European Journal of Dental Education (2020) found that over 90% of dental schools in Europe had completely suspended clinical training by April 2020. In the United States, the ADEA reported similar figures. Students in their final clinical years faced the genuine possibility of delayed graduation. That wasn't a minor inconvenience - it was a workforce pipeline problem for countries already short on practicing dentists.

I've seen disruption before. The 1979 period in Iran reshaped everything about how we practiced and taught. But that disruption played out over months and years. This was a single week in March when everything stopped simultaneously and globally. That's a different kind of shock entirely.

Simulation and Teledentistry Stepped In, With Real Limitations

The response from dental schools took two main forms: expanded simulation-based training and a rapid pivot toward teledentistry education. Simulation labs had existed before 2020, of course, but they'd always been positioned as preparation for the clinic, not a replacement for it. Suddenly institutions were asking whether typodont models and virtual reality simulators could carry more of the load. Some schools that already had VR dental simulators - the Simodont Dental Trainer, for example, used at several European institutions - reported increased hours of use almost immediately.

Teledentistry moved even faster. It wasn't new, but its adoption had been sluggish. CDC telehealth data from 2020 showed that telemedicine visits in the US increased by roughly 154% in the last week of March 2020 compared to the same period in 2019, and dentistry followed that curve where it could - triage, consultation, case review, and patient communication all shifted online. For dental schools, this meant students were learning to assess patients through video calls, which is a genuinely useful clinical skill that had never been formally taught before.

But here's the honest limitation: you cannot teach tactile feedback through a screen. Scaling, cavity preparation, impression technique - these require hands on instruments and instruments on tissue. Simulation helps. It doesn't replicate. And I think some of the early enthusiasm for virtual dental education slightly overestimated how much of clinical competence can be built without physical repetition. Most programs acknowledged this by mid-year and began planning modified re-opening protocols with enhanced PPE rather than treating digital tools as a full substitute.

What Dental Educators Actually Changed and Why Some of It Should Stay

One underreported development in 2020 was the acceleration of case-based learning in online formats. With students unable to see patients, faculty at several institutions began building detailed virtual patient libraries - recorded case presentations with radiographs, clinical photographs, treatment planning discussions, and outcome data. This is something I'd argued for in a different context for years: students learn diagnosis pattern recognition through volume, and a well-curated case library gives them access to presentations they might not see in five years of clinical rotations.

The literature supported this shift. A study by Garg et al. published in the Journal of Dental Education (2020) found that students who engaged with structured virtual case presentations showed diagnostic reasoning scores comparable to traditional clinic-based cohorts, though the authors were careful to note the limitations of self-reported competency measures. That's an important caveat - and one worth taking seriously before anyone concludes that screen-based learning equals clinical learning.

Okay, I'll admit something here that I probably shouldn't put in print but will anyway. A colleague of mine, Dr. Farshid, who teaches at a university I won't name, told me in a phone call sometime around June 2020 that his department had set up a required online module on infection control that students had to complete before returning to clinic. He said three of his most clinically talented students failed it twice because it was timed and they kept getting distracted by their phones. Meanwhile a student who struggled with every hands-on procedure passed it on the first try with what Farshid described as 'suspiciously perfect answers.' He laughed when he told me. I'm not sure that story has a clean lesson, but it stuck with me. Assessment design in dental education is not a solved problem, and 2020 exposed that.

Where I think the 2020 changes should stay permanently: online case libraries, asynchronous lecture delivery, and formal teledentistry communication training. These belong in every dental curriculum going forward, not as emergency measures but as standard tools. The students who graduate having learned to conduct a thorough remote consultation will be more effective practitioners. That's not a pandemic lesson - it's just true.

What 2020 Revealed About Infection Control Education That We Should Have Fixed Earlier

There's an uncomfortable truth behind the 2020 disruption in dental education: the field was not well-prepared to explain its own infection control standards to students, patients, or the public. When patients started asking whether dentistry was safe, many recent graduates struggled to give a clear, evidence-based answer. That's a curriculum failure, not a pandemic surprise.

The CDC's Guidelines for Infection Control in Dental Health-Care Settings have existed since 2003 and were updated in subsequent years. They're thorough. But my experience reviewing junior practitioners in my region suggests that infection control training in many programs had become box-ticking rather than genuine understanding. Students could pass a test on sterilization cycles without being able to explain to a nervous patient why the procedure room was safe. That gap matters clinically and it matters for public trust.

2020 forced a corrective. Schools began building more explicit communication training around infection control - not just the protocols themselves, but how to explain them clearly. And this connects directly to patient behavior at home: when patients understand the science behind clinical hygiene, they're more receptive to guidance on their own habits, including something as foundational as correcting harmful brushing technique and pressure before it causes irreversible damage. Education at the chair depends on the patient trusting the clinician's judgment, and that trust was under pressure in 2020 in ways it hadn't been before.

So what does 2020 actually mean for dental clinical education going forward? It means the field can no longer treat digital literacy, patient communication, and infection control reasoning as secondary competencies. They're core. Any program that still treats clinical hours as the only measure of readiness is working from an incomplete model of what a prepared dentist actually looks like.

If you're an educator or a practitioner involved in training, take one concrete step: review whether your curriculum includes formal instruction in patient communication about infection control - not just the protocols, but how to explain them. If it doesn't, build that in now. 2020 showed us exactly where that gap lives.
